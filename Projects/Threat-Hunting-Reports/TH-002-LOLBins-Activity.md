# TH-002 - Threat Hunting: LOLBins Activity (PowerShell spawning cmd.exe)

## Executive Summary

A threat hunting activity was conducted to determine whether the execution of **cmd.exe** launched by **PowerShell** represented an isolated event or part of a broader pattern of suspicious activity within the monitored environment.

The investigation expanded beyond the initial alert by reviewing PowerShell executions, command shell activity, affected hosts, users involved and related detections generated by Wazuh.

The results confirmed that the observed behavior corresponded to a controlled laboratory simulation and no additional indicators suggesting malicious activity or lateral movement were identified.

---

# Objective

Determine whether PowerShell spawning **cmd.exe** represents isolated administrative activity or a potential attack pattern requiring escalation.

---

# Hunting Hypothesis

Attackers frequently abuse LOLBins (Living Off the Land Binaries) such as **cmd.exe** and **PowerShell** to execute commands while blending into legitimate operating system activity.

Hypothesis:

> If the detected cmd.exe execution is malicious, additional executions or related suspicious behaviors should also exist within the monitored endpoint.

# Technical Analysis

The investigation focused on the relationship between PowerShell and cmd.exe, a common parent-child process combination frequently abused by attackers during post-exploitation activities.

Sysmon Event ID 1 provided sufficient telemetry to reconstruct the execution chain, including:

- Parent process
- Child process
- User
- Process identifiers
- Command line
- Integrity level

Although PowerShell spawning cmd.exe may occur during legitimate administrative tasks, it is also associated with attacker tradecraft and therefore requires contextual validation.

No additional suspicious process chains or repeated executions were identified.

---

# Threat Hunting Assessment

Based on the available telemetry:

- The activity was limited to a single endpoint.
- The execution was initiated by the laboratory user.
- Only one native detection was generated.
- No additional indicators suggested compromise.

The evidence supports classifying the activity as an expected laboratory simulation.

# Lessons Learned

- Parent-child process relationships provide valuable context during investigations.
- LOLBins should always be analyzed within their execution context.
- Threat Hunting extends beyond individual alerts by searching for related activity across the environment.
- Native Wazuh detections provide an effective starting point for proactive investigations.

---

# Recommendations

- Continue monitoring PowerShell spawning LOLBins.
- Correlate process execution with network connections.
- Review Script Block Logging when available.
- Investigate repeated parent-child relationships involving administrative utilities.
- Expand hunting activities to additional LOLBins such as certutil.exe, rundll32.exe and regsvr32.exe.

---

# Conclusion

The threat hunting activity confirmed that the observed execution of cmd.exe launched by PowerShell corresponded to a controlled laboratory exercise.

No evidence of persistence, lateral movement or additional suspicious activity was identified.

The investigation demonstrates how a single alert can be expanded into a broader hunting exercise, validating both telemetry quality and the effectiveness of native Wazuh detections while reinforcing investigative methodology applicable to SOC and Threat Hunting operations.
